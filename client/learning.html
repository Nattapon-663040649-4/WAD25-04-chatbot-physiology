The Human Heart: Structure and Function

The human heart is a remarkable muscular organ approximately the size of a closed fist, serving as the central pump of the cardiovascular system. Located in the mediastinum between the lungs, slightly left of the body's midline, the heart beats approximately 100,000 times per day, pumping about 5 liters of blood per minute at rest, and up to 25 liters during intense physical activity.

The heart's primary function is to maintain continuous blood circulation throughout the body, delivering oxygen and nutrients to tissues while removing carbon dioxide and metabolic waste products. This dual circulatory system consists of pulmonary circulation, which carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs for oxygenation, and systemic circulation, which distributes oxygenated blood to all body tissues.

Cardiac Anatomy Overview

The heart consists of four chambers working in coordinated sequence to maintain efficient blood circulation. The cardiac cycle, lasting approximately 0.8 seconds at rest, involves systematic contraction and relaxation of these chambers.

Four-Chamber Structure

Right Atrium: Receives deoxygenated blood from the body through the superior and inferior vena cavae. This thin-walled chamber operates at low pressure (0-8 mmHg) and contracts during atrial systole to complete ventricular filling.

Right Ventricle: Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery. Its walls are thicker than the atria but thinner than the left ventricle, as it pumps against lower pulmonary vascular resistance.

Left Atrium: Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs through four pulmonary veins. It has slightly thicker walls than the right atrium due to higher pressure requirements.

Left Ventricle: The heart's most muscular chamber, with walls 8-12mm thick. It generates systolic pressures of 120 mmHg to pump oxygenated blood throughout the entire body via the aorta.

Cardiac Septum

The interventricular septum is a thick muscular wall separating the left and right ventricles, preventing mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. It contains part of the cardiac conduction system and contributes to ventricular contraction efficiency.

The Human Brain: Structure and Function

The human brain, weighing approximately 1.4 kilograms (about 2% of body weight), serves as the command center of the nervous system. This complex organ contains approximately 86 billion neurons and an equal number of glial cells, forming intricate networks responsible for all cognitive functions, sensory processing, motor control, and homeostatic regulation.

Protected by the skull and three layers of meninges (dura mater, arachnoid mater, and pia mater), the brain floats in cerebrospinal fluid, which provides cushioning and nutrient transport. Despite its small mass, the brain consumes approximately 20% of the body's total oxygen and glucose, reflecting its intense metabolic activity.

Cerebral Cortex

The cerebral cortex, the brain's outer layer of grey matter, is approximately 2-4mm thick and highly folded to increase surface area. It contains 14-16 billion neurons organized in six distinct layers (I-VI), each with specific cellular compositions and connection patterns.

Hemispheric Lateralization

The cerebrum is divided into left and right hemispheres, connected by the corpus callosum containing approximately 200-250 million axons. While both hemispheres process all types of information, certain functions show lateralization:

  • Left Hemisphere: Typically dominant for language, analytical thinking, and sequential processing
  • Right Hemisphere: Specialized for spatial awareness, facial recognition, music perception, and emotional processing
